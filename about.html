Is Minesweeper Educational?

  • Mathematicians are starting to get excited about the computer game Minesweeper, included with Windows operating systems. Richard Kaye, a math professor in England, realized that a solution to a larger scale version of Minesweeper would be the solution to a 30-year-old mathematics conundrum known as the “P vs. NP” problem. To “celebrate mathematics in the new millennium,” the Clay Mathematics Institute of Cambridge, Massachusetts, announced its Millennium Prizes last May, offering US$1 million each for the correct solution to each of 7 famous, unsolved math problems. “P vs. NP” is on that list.
  • The object of Minesweeper is to avoid all the hidden mines on a square grid. When you click on a grid, you either find a mine (and lose) or get a number showing how many mines are in the squares around the one you just clicked. Kaye realized that this was exactly the kind of problem described by Stephen Cook back in 1971 and now referred to as P vs. NP. In very simplistic terms, the P vs. NP problem is described by the Clay Institute as follows:
  • One of the outstanding problems in logic and computer science is determining whether questions exist whose answer can be quickly checked (for example by computer), but which require a much longer time to solve from scratch (without knowing the answer). There certainly seem to be many such questions. But so far no one has proved that any of them really does require a long time to solve …
  • If someone were to come up with an algorithm for successfully beating Minesweeper, that algorithm would also be instrumental in vastly improving encryption and code-cracking.
  • For more information, please see the Associated Press item. For a better explanation of the P vs. NP problem, see The Clay Mathematics Institute's explanations.

It's the same with learning patterns--after three years of playing Minesweeper a few hours a day, you don't have to think about where the mines are when you see these patterns.

  • Use Flags Wisely

    If you play using flags, employ the "double-click" technique (press the left and right mouse buttons at the same time). This action opens all squares surrounding a square with a number, if you have marked the number of mines around that number.
